A message from the founder

Learn-DA was inspired by a simple but powerful realization: accurate forecasts fundamentally change how we understand and interact with the world.

During my time as a Ph.D. student, I experienced firsthand how confusing these concepts can feel initially. Terms like background, analysis, uncertainty, localization, and inflation appear abstract, and the connection between theory and real-world systems is not always clear. That experience shaped the vision behind Learn-DA.

Learn-DA was created to bridge that gap. The platform brings together operational forecasting, scientific computing, and hands-on learning in a unified environment — enabling students, researchers, and industry teams to access reliable atmospheric forecasts and understand how those forecasts are generated and continuously improved using observations.

The goal is simple: make advanced forecasting systems more transparent, accessible, and usable — for everyone from undergraduates discovering the basics to PhD students developing research-grade methods.

— Elías D. Niño-Ruiz, Ph.D.

Theory

The science behind the platform

Data assimilation combines a dynamical model with observations to estimate the true state of a system while explicitly tracking uncertainty. The update step blends both sources in a principled, mathematically rigorous way.

→

Repeat

In a classic linear-Gaussian setting, the analysis update and Kalman gain are:

\[ x_a = x_b + K\,(y - Hx_b), \qquad K = P_b H^\top \left(H P_b H^\top + R\right)^{-1}. \]
\(x_b\)
Background state (model forecast prior to update)
\(x_a\)
Analysis state (corrected estimate after assimilation)
\(y\)
Observations from sensors, satellites, or stations
\(H\)
Observation operator mapping model state to obs space
\(K\)
Kalman gain — optimal weight balancing model vs obs
\(R\)
Observation error covariance (measurement uncertainty)

Ensemble methods like EnKF and LETKF approximate covariances using an ensemble of model runs, applying localization and inflation for stable, scalable performance in real systems.
